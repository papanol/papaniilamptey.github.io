HOUSEHOLD EXPENSE ANALYSIS

The objective of this project is to determine the impact of smoking and other personal attributes on expenses through linear and multilinear regression modeling.

  • View Code

Scenario
Mr. John Hughes has been gathering data in an effort to comprehend how individual characteristics impact household spending. He collects information, on factors such, as age, gender, smoking habits, BMI, number of children, location, and ultimately their expenditures.

Goal
This project aims to determine the impact of smoking and other personal attributes on expenses through linear and multilinear regression modeling. Additionally, we aim to examine how factors, including age, smoking habits, BMI, gender, number of children, and region can influence household expenditures using multivariate linear regression.

Data Overview

The dataset named "MultiRegDataset.csv" holds data that Mr. John Hughes gathered regarding how personal attributes impact household expenses. It consists of 1355 observations (rows) and 7 features (columns) encompassing variables such as age, sex, BMI, children, smoker, region, and expenses.
Dependent variable: Expenses
Independent variables: Age, Sex, BMI, Children, Smoker, Region

Key Statistics

Expense Histogram

The distribution of expenses appears to be positively skewed, as the tail of the histogram extends towards higher values. The mean is greater than the median.

T-Test
Hypothesis Statement
Null hypothesis H₀: μexpenses= 15000
The mean for expenses is equal to 15000
Alternative hypothesis Ha: μexpenses ≠ 15000
The mean for expenses is not equal to 15000

The one-sample t-test has a p-value of 2.092e-06 which is less than the significance level of 0.05. This gives us evidence to reject the null hypothesis that the mean for expenses is equal to $15000.

Recommendation
Simple Linear Regression (Smoker vs. Expenses):
The analysis shows a connection between smoking habits and household expenses. Smokers tend to have higher expenses as compared to non-smokers. The model explains about 62.48% of the difference in expenses and the p value for the smoker coefficient is zero confirming that this relationship is significant.
Multiple Linear Regression (All Variables vs. Expenses): In general factors such, as smoking habits, age, region,body mass index (BMI) and the number of children play a role in determining household expenses. The multiple regression model used in this study successfully explains around 75.36% of the differences in expenses suggesting that it has an ability to predict. These findings offer insights into understanding the factors that influence variations, in household spending.
